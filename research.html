Working Papers

Globalization and Labor Market Outcomes in China
with Prof. Shu LIN

Using China’s WTO accession as a natural experiment, we show that input tariff liberalization generates more jobs and higher incomes for manufacturing workers but also widens gender employment and income gaps. The widened gender income gap is not due to a longer working time by men but instead a large increase in gender wage inequality. We also find that input tariff reductions worsen Chinese manufacturing workers' health, and this adverse effect is more pronounced for women. Further analysis reveals that the worsening of gender disparities applies mainly to the unskilled, which incentivizes women to obtain more education.
